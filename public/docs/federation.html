Federation

Federation enables independent Weblisk instances to discover, trust, and collaborate across organizational boundaries. Services publish capabilities to a shared registry and consume them through verified, governance-compliant channels.

How Federation Works

Federation follows a four-step flow that connects independent services while maintaining security and governance guarantees:

  1. Publish — A service publishes its blueprint to a federation registry, declaring its capabilities, version, and visibility scope.
  2. Discover — Other services discover it via the registry or direct peering, filtering by capability, provider, or version constraints.
  3. Verify — Trust is established through ML-DSA-65 signature verification. Each published blueprint carries a cryptographic signature that consumers validate before establishing connections.
  4. Communicate — Cross-service calls respect both parties' governance policies. The strictest policy always applies, ensuring neither party's security requirements are violated.

Registry

The federation registry is a decentralized catalog where services advertise their capabilities. Services publish what they offer and declare what they need. The registry handles matchmaking, version resolution, and availability tracking.

yaml 
federation:
  registry: https://registry.weblisk.dev
  publish:
    - name: todo-api
      version: 1.0.0
      capabilities: [todos.read, todos.write]
      visibility: public
  discover:
    - name: auth-service
      provider: acme-corp
      min_version: 2.0.0

Trust Model

Federation uses a web-of-trust approach. Each participant signs their published blueprints with ML-DSA-65 keys. Consumers verify signatures before establishing connections, ensuring that only authentic, untampered services are consumed.

Trust can be established through three mechanisms:

  • Direct trust — Explicit key exchange between two parties. The most secure option, requiring out-of-band verification but providing the strongest guarantees.
  • Registry-mediated trust — The registry vouches for participants. Services trust the registry's verification process, which includes identity validation and capability auditing.
  • Transitive trust — Trusted by a trusted party. If you trust Service A and Service A trusts Service B, transitive trust allows connecting to Service B. Depth is configurable to limit trust chains.
Trust Type Security Setup Effort Scalability
Direct Highest High Low
Registry-mediated High Medium High
Transitive Medium Low Very high

Cross-Service Communication

Federated services communicate via standard protocols chosen for their security and performance characteristics:

  • HTTP/2 with mTLS — For synchronous request/response calls. Mutual TLS ensures both parties authenticate each other.
  • WebTransport — For streaming and bidirectional real-time communication with lower overhead than WebSocket.
  • NATS/JetStream — For asynchronous messaging, event streaming, and durable pub/sub across service boundaries.

To consume a federated service, declare it as a dependency in your blueprint:

yaml 
dependencies:
  auth:
    federation: acme-corp/auth-service@^2.0
    capabilities: [auth.verify, auth.refresh]
    fallback: local-jwt

The fallback field specifies a local implementation to use when the federated service is unreachable, ensuring graceful degradation without hard dependencies on external systems.

Governance Across Boundaries

When services federate, the strictest governance tier applies. If your service operates at Tier 3 and the remote service operates at Tier 4, the connection operates at Tier 4. This prevents governance downgrade attacks where a less-regulated service could bypass security controls by routing through a permissive intermediary.

Governance enforcement is bidirectional — both parties must satisfy the higher tier's requirements before communication proceeds. This includes:

  • Signature verification at the required algorithm strength
  • Audit logging meeting the higher tier's retention and detail requirements
  • Rate limiting conforming to the stricter of both parties' policies
  • Data handling respecting the most restrictive data boundary rules

Mesh Topologies

Federation supports three mesh topologies, each with distinct trade-offs for latency, resilience, and management overhead:

Topology Discovery Latency Resilience Management
Hub-and-spoke Central registry Medium Low (single point of failure) Simple
Peer-to-peer Direct discovery Low High Complex
Hybrid Registry + direct peering Low High Moderate

Hub-and-spoke centralizes discovery through a single registry. Simple to manage and reason about, but the registry becomes a single point of failure. Best for smaller federations with a clear organizational center.

Peer-to-peer eliminates the central registry entirely. Services discover each other through direct key exchange and gossip protocols. Maximum resilience but higher operational complexity. Suited for highly distributed or adversarial environments.

Hybrid combines registry-based discovery with direct peering for frequently-accessed services. The registry bootstraps initial connections, then services establish direct channels for performance-critical paths. Recommended for most production deployments.
